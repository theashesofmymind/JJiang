As a conceptual artist, I work on the visibility of the human mental body in the future knowledge system, particularly with impending technological advancement. In the post-industrial era, I am intrigued by where creativity, vulnerability, and ephemerality reside in knowledge and in what situations their power can be activated. My works manifest through site-specific performances and sculptures.

Growing up with incessant family travels and being educated internationally, I developed a keen sensitivity to materials that construct realities. Simultaneously, I recognized the absence of the human mental body in knowledge construction. Considering reality as a game, I employ instruction, artifacts, and (human) reaction as 3 components of a cyclical model to approach the complexities of Anthropocene existence and their interconnected relationships. This model as an imagining tool helps me reflect on the order imposed on the shared reality, conditioned language, and existing knowledge categorizations.

‘A North Pole Iceberg’ is a performance that embodies this approach. Described as ‘a 200m long, 80m tall iceberg,’ the piece is ultimately revealed as a holdable, pacifier-shaped piece of ice after following a series of instructions. The performer concludes with eating the ice and expelling a piece of bone. Also, my sculptures connect human bodies and artifacts by visualizing gravity, scale, materiality, and decay at site-specific locations. I cast a flesh-toned silicone apple filled with expired pills and affixed it to a fruit tree in a forest using an arrow. I created a scarred Band-Aid from a human bedding set which was placed in eight libraries, eliciting diverse responses from visitors and guards. They are triggers to glimpse the hidden facets of realities and the interior side of a gaming body.

Looking forward, I envision further research into the history of narrative technology and museum archives. I aim to delve deeper into the nature of creativity and how it will be embodied in the future human body and knowledge systems. I consider my work as creative speculations on the meaning of knowledge.
